Select the plan type and matching structure that best fits your company goals.
Plan Type
Recommended for most small businesses. Safe Harbor plans are automatically exempt from ADP/ACP nondiscrimination testing
What is ADP/ACP testing? Each year, the IRS requires 401(k) plans to verify that highly compensated employees (HCEs — those earning $160,000+ in 2026) are not contributing at a disproportionately higher rate than non-highly compensated employees (NHCEs). If they are, the plan fails the test and refunds must be issued to HCEs by March 15. Safe Harbor plans are permanently exempt, eliminating this risk entirely.
— the most common compliance headache for small 401(k) plans.
Basic Match
Recommended Most Common
100% of first 3% + 50% of next 2% = max 4% employer match
Example: Employee earns $75,000/year and defers 5%.
Match = 100% × $2,250 + 50% × $750 = $2,625/year employer contribution
  1. Automatically satisfies ADP/ACP nondiscrimination tests — eliminates the most common compliance risk for small plans.
  2. Safe Harbor contributions vest immediately — employees own them on day one, improving participation rates significantly.
  3. Must be communicated to employees at least 30 days before plan year begins. FHT handles all required notices automatically.
Enhanced Match
Retention
100% of first 4–6% of compensation — higher match to attract and retain top talent
Example: Employee earns $75,000/year and defers 6%.
With 100% match on 6%: $4,500/year employer contribution
  1. Any match at or above the basic Safe Harbor formula satisfies ADP/ACP testing — full protection with a more generous formula.
  2. Higher match rates significantly improve employee participation and average deferral rates — critical for HCE compliance.
  3. Enhanced match is fully tax-deductible as a business expense, up to 25% of eligible employee compensation.
3% Non-Elective
All Employees
3% of compensation contributed to ALL eligible employees regardless of whether they contribute themselves
Example: Employee earns $75,000/year and contributes $0.
Employer still contributes $2,250/year on their behalf.
  1. Can be adopted as late as 30 days before year-end — useful if you discover an ADP/ACP testing failure that needs retroactive correction.
  2. All eligible employees receive the contribution whether or not they participate — can increase costs if participation is low.
  3. SECURE 2.0 allows non-elective contributions to be designated as Roth, giving employees additional tax flexibility.
No Match
Flexible
Employee-only contributions. Safe Harbor testing exemption requires 3% non-elective if no match is made.
  1. Without a match, participation rates typically drop significantly — especially among non-HCEs whose contributions drive ADP testing results.
  2. If you want Safe Harbor testing protection with no match, a 3% non-elective contribution is required instead.
  3. This option works best for owner-only plans or where all employees are HCEs.

Discretionary Match
Flexible
Set your own match formula each year. Maximum flexibility — employer decides annually how much to match.
  1. Discretionary match can be changed or eliminated each plan year — useful for businesses with variable cash flow.
  2. Annual ADP/ACP testing required — if HCEs contribute more than NHCEs, corrective action may be required by March 15.
  3. Vesting schedules up to 6 years (graded) or 3 years (cliff) are permitted — can be used as a retention tool.
Fixed Formula Match
Cost / Tax
A set formula you commit to every year — e.g. 50% of first 6%. Predictable cost, fully deductible.
  1. Fixed formulas provide predictability for budgeting and are generally better for employee morale than discretionary matches.
  2. Still subject to annual ADP/ACP testing — consider adding a Safe Harbor amendment if testing becomes a concern.
  3. Can be amended prospectively with proper notice — you cannot reduce a match mid-year without a 30-day advance notice period.
Roth 401(k) Option
Tax-Free Growth
Allow employees to make after-tax Roth contributions alongside or instead of traditional pre-tax deferrals. No income limits — anyone can contribute regardless of salary.
Example: Employee earns $200,000 — too high for a Roth IRA.
Can still contribute up to $23,500 as Roth 401(k) → all growth and withdrawals tax-free in retirement
  1. Adding a Roth feature costs nothing — it is a plan design election that allows employees to choose their tax treatment. FHT enables this in your plan document automatically.
  2. SECURE 2.0 now permits employer matching contributions to also be designated as Roth (effective 2024). Employees pay tax on the match now but enjoy tax-free growth forever.
  3. Roth 401(k) has no income limits unlike Roth IRA ($165,000 single / $246,000 married phase-out in 2026) — making it the primary Roth vehicle for high earners.

Solo 401(k) is only available to self-employed individuals and owner-only businesses with no full-time W-2 employees other than the owner and their spouse. Offers the highest contribution limits of any plan for sole proprietors.
Traditional Solo 401(k)
Most Common High Limits
Pre-tax contributions reduce taxable income today. Combine employee deferrals + employer profit sharing for maximum tax-deferred savings.
Example: Self-employed, net income $200,000.
Employee deferral: $23,500 + Employer profit sharing: $45,500 = $69,000 total in 2026
  1. 2026 total limit: $70,000 ($77,500 age 50+, $81,250 age 60–63 per SECURE 2.0) — highest of any plan type for sole proprietors.
  2. Employer profit sharing capped at 25% of W-2 wages or ~20% of net self-employment income after the SE tax deduction.
  3. Must be established by December 31 of the tax year; contributions can be made through the filing deadline including extensions.
Roth Solo 401(k)
Tax-Free Growth High Limits
After-tax deferrals grow completely tax-free. No income limits — anyone can contribute regardless of earnings, unlike Roth IRA.
Example: Owner expecting lower income in retirement.
$23,500 Roth deferral today → $0 tax on all future growth and withdrawals
  1. SECURE 2.0 allows employer profit sharing to also be designated Roth — potentially the entire $70,000 limit can be Roth.
  2. No income limits unlike Roth IRA (phases out at $165,000 single / $246,000 married in 2026). High earners excluded from Roth IRA can still use Roth Solo 401(k).
  3. Qualified distributions (age 59½+, 5-year holding period met) are 100% tax-free including all investment growth.
Traditional + Roth (Split)
Flexible
Split deferrals between pre-tax and Roth. Hedge tax treatment — diversify tax exposure the same way you diversify investments.
  1. The $23,500 employee deferral limit is shared across Traditional and Roth — you cannot contribute the full amount to each. Split as you choose within the combined limit.
  2. Many advisors recommend splitting to hedge against future tax rate changes — diversifying tax treatment the same way you diversify asset classes.
  3. FHT tracks Traditional and Roth sub-accounts separately with annual statements distinguishing Roth basis from pre-tax amounts for accurate IRS reporting.
Hiring employees terminates Solo 401(k) eligibility. If you hire a full-time W-2 employee other than your spouse, the plan must convert to a standard 401(k). FHT will notify you and manage the conversion.
Cash Balance plans are defined benefit plans.
What is a Cash Balance plan?
  1. A Cash Balance plan is a type of defined benefit (pension) plan where each participant has a hypothetical account that grows by a set annual pay credit (e.g., 10% of salary) plus a guaranteed interest credit (e.g., 5%).
  2. Unlike a 401(k), the employer bears all investment risk — the guaranteed interest credit is paid regardless of actual investment returns.
  3. At retirement, participants can take their balance as a lump sum or convert it to a monthly annuity — making Cash Balance the most flexible pension plan structure.
They allow much higher contributions than 401(k) plans — ideal for high-income business owners looking to maximize tax-deferred savings. Requires an enrolled actuary and annual PBGC premiums
What is an enrolled actuary? An IRS-licensed professional who certifies the plan's funding status annually. Required by ERISA for all defined benefit plans — FHT coordinates this service through our actuarial partners.

What are PBGC premiums? The Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC) is a federal agency that insures pension benefits. All defined benefit plans must pay annual premiums — currently $101 per participant for single-employer plans — which FHT files automatically on your behalf.

  2. Contributions to the Cash Balance plan must continue each year (within a range). This plan type is best for employers with stable, high income.
  3. Participants receive a guaranteed hypothetical interest credit (typically 5%) regardless of investment performance — employer bears investment risk.

403(b) plans are only available to public schools, 501(c)(3) non-profits, and certain other tax-exempt organizations. If your organization does not qualify, select a 401(k) or Safe Harbor plan instead.
403(b) with Employer Match
Recommended
Tax-sheltered annuity plan with employer matching. Same contribution limits as 401(k) — $23,500 employee deferral in 2025, plus $7,500 catch-up for age 50+.
Special 15-year catch-up rule: employees with 15+ years of service at the same qualifying employer can contribute an additional $3,000/year (up to $15,000 lifetime).
  1. ERISA-covered 403(b) plans (those with employer contributions) require Form 5500 filing, bonding, and the same ERISA protections as 401(k) plans.
  2. Non-ERISA 403(b) plans (employee-only, no employer match) have simpler compliance requirements but provide no employer tax deduction.
  3. 403(b) plans may invest in annuity contracts or mutual funds — unlike 401(k) plans which have broader investment flexibility.

Employee deferrals only — no employer contribution. Simpler compliance, no Form 5500 required. Common for small non-profits with limited budgets.

457(b) plans are only available to state/local government employers and certain non-profit organizations. Governmental 457(b) plans and non-governmental 457(b) plans have significantly different rules.
Governmental 457(b)
High Limits
For state and local government employers. Can be combined with a 401(k) or 403(b) for double the deferral limit — $47,000 total in 2025.
Government employee can defer $23,500 to a 401(k) or 403(b) AND $23,500 to a 457(b) = $47,000/year total deferral.
  1. Governmental 457(b) assets are held in trust — participants are fully protected in the event of employer bankruptcy, unlike non-governmental 457(b) plans.
  2. Special 3-year catch-up provision: in the 3 years prior to normal retirement age, employees can contribute up to 2x the annual limit ($47,000 in 2025).
  3. No 10% early withdrawal penalty for 457(b) distributions — withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income but not subject to the additional penalty that applies to 401(k) plans.
Non-Governmental 457(b)
Tax-Exempt Orgs Only
For 501(c)(3) and other tax-exempt non-profits. Available only to a select group of highly compensated employees or top management.
  1. Non-governmental 457(b) assets remain employer property until distributed — participants are unsecured creditors in the event of employer insolvency.
  2. Distributions can only occur upon separation from service, an unforeseeable emergency, or plan termination — no hardship or loan provisions.
  3. Cannot be combined with a 401(k) for double deferral (unlike governmental 457(b)) — the $23,500 limit applies across all plans.
Multiple Employer Plans (MEPs) allow unrelated employers to join a single 401(k) plan. Each employer maintains independent plan decisions while sharing administrative costs. MEPs are ideal for industry associations, professional groups, or franchises wanting to offer a competitive benefit at lower cost.
Standard MEP
Most Common Cost Sharing
Multiple unrelated employers share a single plan document and administrative infrastructure. Each employer retains independent control over their match formula, vesting, and eligibility.
Example: A regional trade association sponsors a MEP for its 12 member businesses. Each business sets its own match but shares recordkeeping costs — saving each employer $2,000–$5,000/year vs. a standalone plan.
  1. Each employer files its own Form 5500, but the plan administrator (FHT) handles all filings on behalf of the group. This significantly reduces administrative burden for small employers.
  2. A "bad apple" rule historically applied — if one employer violated plan rules, all employers could be disqualified. SECURE Act eliminated this risk for MEPs that meet certain requirements.
  3. Employers can exit a MEP by spinning off their employees into a standalone plan — giving full flexibility if the employer's needs change.
Safe Harbor MEP
Testing Exempt
MEP with Safe Harbor design — all participating employers are exempt from ADP/ACP nondiscrimination testing. Requires standardized match formula across employers.
  1. Safe Harbor MEPs require all participating employers to use the same match formula — typically 100% of first 3% or basic match. This simplifies administration but reduces individual employer flexibility.
  2. All Safe Harbor contributions vest immediately across all participating employers — there is no variation in vesting schedules permitted.
  3. FHT acts as the Named Fiduciary for the MEP, significantly reducing ERISA liability exposure for each participating employer.
Pooled Employer Plans (PEPs) are a new plan type created by the SECURE Act (2019). A Pooled Plan Provider (PPP) like FHT sponsors and administers the plan — employers simply participate. PEPs offer the lowest administrative burden of any multi-employer structure.
FHT Pooled Employer Plan
Recommended Lowest Admin Burden
FHT serves as the Pooled Plan Provider (PPP) — a registered fiduciary. One Form 5500 is filed for the entire pool. Employers retain control over their own match, vesting, and eligibility within FHT's approved menu of options.
Example: A 15-person professional services firm joins FHT's PEP. They pay no plan setup fee, file no Form 5500 independently, and have zero ERISA bonding requirements — saving $3,000–$8,000/year in administrative costs.
  1. FHT as PPP files a single Form 5500 for all participating employers — eliminating one of the most burdensome annual compliance obligations for small plan sponsors.
  2. PEP employers are not required to obtain a fidelity bond (normally required for all ERISA plans) — FHT's bond covers all participants in the pool.
  3. Employers can join a PEP mid-year and exit at any time by spinning off their employees into a standalone plan. There is no lock-in period or exit penalty.
FHT Safe Harbor PEP
Testing Exempt Lowest Admin Burden
Safe Harbor version of the FHT PEP — all employers are exempt from ADP/ACP testing. Standardized Safe Harbor match applies across all participating employers.
  1. The Safe Harbor PEP combines the lowest administrative burden of a PEP with the compliance simplicity of Safe Harbor — eliminating both nondiscrimination testing AND independent Form 5500 filing.
  2. SECURE 2.0 expanded PEP eligibility — any employer, regardless of size or industry, can now join a PEP. Previously there were commonality requirements.
  3. FHT as Pooled Plan Provider assumes significant fiduciary responsibility for investment selection and plan compliance — dramatically reducing individual employer ERISA exposure.

Nondiscrimination Testing Notice
The plan you've selected is subject to the annual IRS nondiscrimination testing. Passing these tests is based on which employees participate and how much the employer and employees contribute to the plan. The IRS wants to avoid highly compensated individuals from gaining an unfair tax advantage so we will perform the required testing for the client each year.
If the plan fails the test the employer may have to adjust the contributions to the highly compensated individuals or refund portions of their 401(k) contributions.

QDIA: Vanguard Target Date funds serve as your plan's Qualified Default Investment Alternative — protecting you from fiduciary liability for participants who don't make an investment election. What is a QDIA?
A QDIA (Qualified Default Investment Alternative) is the fund your plan automatically invests in when an employee doesn't make an investment choice. By using a DOL-approved QDIA like a target date fund, you are protected from fiduciary liability for the investment performance of that default — one of the most important protections available to plan sponsors.

Vesting Schedule
How quickly do employees take ownership of employer contributions?

What is Profit Sharing?
What is profit sharing?
1What it is: A profit sharing contribution is a discretionary employer contribution to employees' 401(k) accounts — separate from the regular match. The employer decides each year whether to contribute and how much, based on company performance.
2Why employers do it: Profit sharing aligns employee interests with company success, boosts morale, and creates a powerful tax deduction — contributions are fully deductible up to 25% of total eligible payroll. If it's a good year, the company can contribute. If not, they don't have to.
3Benefits for employees: Profit sharing grows tax-deferred inside the 401(k), meaning employees don't pay taxes until withdrawal. It's essentially free money on top of their own savings — and can add thousands of dollars per year to their retirement balance without any action on their part.

INVESTMENT POLICY STATEMENT

Purpose: This Investment Policy Statement establishes the investment objectives, guidelines, and procedures for the retirement plan established by your company. It is intended to provide a framework for prudent investment management consistent with ERISA fiduciary standards.

Investment Objectives: To provide plan participants with a diversified menu of investment options sufficient to allow them to construct portfolios consistent with their individual risk tolerance and time horizon.

Default Investment (QDIA): Vanguard Target Retirement Fund Series. Participants who do not make an investment election will be automatically invested in the age-appropriate Target Date fund.

Investment Menu: Vanguard Target Retirement Series (QDIA) · Vanguard Total Stock Market Index · Vanguard 500 Index · Vanguard Small-Cap Index · Vanguard Total International Stock Index · Vanguard Total Bond Market Index · Vanguard Short-Term Bond Index · Vanguard Real Estate Index · Vanguard Federal Money Market

Monitoring Standards: FHT will conduct quarterly performance reviews of all investment options. Funds will be evaluated against their respective benchmark indices and peer group averages. Funds that underperform for 4 consecutive quarters will be placed on a watch list and evaluated for replacement.

Fund Selection Criteria: Funds shall be evaluated based on: (1) performance relative to benchmark over 1, 3, and 5-year periods; (2) expense ratio relative to peer group; (3) manager tenure and organizational stability; (4) Morningstar rating and risk-adjusted returns; (5) fund assets and liquidity.

Watch List Criteria: A fund will be placed on the Watch List if it underperforms its benchmark by more than 2% annualized over any rolling 4-quarter period, if its expense ratio exceeds the 75th percentile of its peer group, or if there is a significant change in fund management or investment strategy.

Removal Criteria: A fund on the Watch List for two consecutive quarterly reviews without improvement shall be recommended for replacement. The Plan Sponsor will be notified in writing and given 30 days to provide direction before a replacement fund is implemented.

QDIA Monitoring: The Vanguard Target Date Series serving as QDIA shall be reviewed annually for glide path appropriateness, fee competitiveness, and performance relative to the Morningstar Target Date category average.

Documentation: FHT shall maintain written records of all investment reviews, fund changes, and fiduciary decisions. These records shall be available to the Plan Sponsor upon request and retained for a minimum of 6 years.
